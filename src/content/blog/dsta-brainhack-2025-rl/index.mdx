## The competition

**[DSTA Brainhack 2025](https://www.dsta.gov.sg/brainhack)** is not your typical weekend hackathon. Over roughly **six weeks**, teams compete across three tracks — computer vision, automatic speech recognition, and reinforcement learning. My team was Richie, Javier, Jia Yu, and me. Javier and Jia Yu anchored the CV and ASR challenges. Richie and I owned the RL track.

The goal of the RL challenge sounded deceptively simple: navigate a grid map, collect as many points as possible, and do not get caught by the guards. The catch — your agent can only see within a limited **viewcone**. You are always partially blind. Guards move. The map does not explain itself. And you only have however many training runs you can squeeze in before the clock runs out.

We made it to the **semifinals, finishing in the top 16** nationally. We did not make the final cut. But the journey of getting there — especially the night before submission — is the part I keep thinking about.

## Six weeks that felt like one long sprint

The competition gave us time, but time spent iterating on an RL agent has a way of evaporating. Every approach feels promising until it fails in a way you did not anticipate, and then you spend a day diagnosing the failure before you earn the right to try something new.

Early on I worked largely independently, running experiments and reading papers between other commitments. The environment's partial observability made it immediately clear that vanilla approaches would struggle: if your agent cannot remember where it has been, it will circle back into guards it already encountered.

What made this especially brutal was containerisation. **Docker** was our deployment target — every submission had to run cleanly inside a container, which meant environment setup, dependency pinning, and reproduction bugs were a constant tax on top of the actual research work. Getting a training loop working on your machine and then watching it silently fail inside a container at 2 a.m. is a specific kind of pain.

## Four approaches, three dead ends

### Vanilla PPO

The natural starting point. Proximal Policy Optimisation with a standard feedforward network. It trained fast, was well-understood, and failed in a very predictable way: the agent had no memory. Every timestep was evaluated in isolation. It learned to react but not to *reason*. Guards it had seen ten steps ago were invisible to it now. The performance plateau came quickly.

**What it taught me:** memoryless policies are fundamentally inadequate for environments with partial observability. You are not solving the right problem.

### BFS

Stepping back from learned policies entirely, I tried classical pathfinding. BFS finds the shortest path to a known objective — and it did exactly that. Deterministically. Efficiently. Without any ability to adapt when a guard moved into the path it had computed.

The issue is that BFS assumes a static world. Our world was adversarial. Guards do not care about your planned route.

**What it taught me:** optimal in a known world, brittle in an unknown one.

### A\*

A natural upgrade from BFS — heuristic pathfinding with Manhattan distance to the goal. Better efficiency, same core problem. A* navigated more cleanly toward objectives but was just as blind to dynamic threats. You can have perfect goal-oriented movement and still walk straight into a guard.

**What it taught me:** smarter pathfinding is not a substitute for learning. The adversarial component cannot be heuristically patched around.

### PPO + LSTM — the one that worked

The breakthrough came when we paired PPO with an **LSTM layer** between the input and the policy/value heads:

```
Input (Viewcone + State) → LSTM(128) → Dense(64) → Policy Head
                                                   → Value Head
```

The LSTM carries a hidden state across timesteps. It gave the agent something it had never had before: **memory**. Not a map in the traditional sense, but a compressed, learned representation of what it had observed over time. Suddenly the agent could anticipate where guards might be based on where they *were*. It could remember which areas it had already swept. It could make decisions that were not purely reactive.

The performance delta was immediately visible — higher points per episode, lower capture rate, more purposeful navigation patterns.

## Reward engineering: the part nobody talks about enough

Getting the architecture right was half the battle. The reward function was the other half, and it required the kind of obsessive iteration that only makes sense at 3 a.m.

The failure modes were specific and frustrating:

- The agent became **too conservative** — it found a safe corner and sat there, technically not getting caught, technically scoring nothing useful.
- It fell into **local optima** — find one point, return to start, repeat forever.
- **Back-and-forth oscillation** — the agent would shuffle between two tiles endlessly rather than committing to a direction.

Each failure mode required a targeted intervention. The final reward structure that shipped:

```python
reward = {
    'point_collection':       +50,   # Prioritise the actual objective
    'step_penalty':           -0.5,  # Punish aimless wandering
    'capture_penalty':        -200,  # Capture is catastrophic
    'distance_to_point':      -0.2,  # Nudge agent toward objectives
    'guard_proximity_penalty': -5,   # Discourage unnecessary risk
    'exploration_bonus':       +2,   # Reward new tile discovery
    'wall_collision':          -1,   # No valid reason to hit walls
}
```

The `capture_penalty` being 4× the `point_collection` reward sounds aggressive, but anything softer and the agent treated capture as an acceptable trade-off for a scoring opportunity. It needed to genuinely fear being caught.

## The night before

We did not sleep before the semifinals submission.

By the evening prior, the PPO+LSTM agent was trained and running — but not well enough. The submission window was a fixed deadline. Whatever we uploaded was what the judges would evaluate. Richie and I spent the entire night running variants: adjusting reward weights, tweaking LSTM hidden size, trying different training durations, and diagnosing why certain configurations that looked good in local eval performed inconsistently on the judge's environment.

Docker made every iteration slower. A change to the training script meant rebuilding the container, re-running, extracting the artefact, repackaging. By 4 a.m. we had a system that worked, but "works" in RL means "performs well enough on average" — and you never fully know until the evaluation runs on their infrastructure.

We submitted. We waited.

## Top 16 — and what I'd do differently

We made the semifinals. **Top 16 nationally** is a result I'm proud of, especially given that the RL track is the least formulaic of the three — CV and ASR have clearer solution templates; RL requires you to actually figure out the environment.

We did not make the finals cut. Looking back, the things I would change:

1. **Start LSTM earlier.** We spent too long trying to extract value from memoryless architectures. The moment partial observability is a constraint, memory should be the first hypothesis.
2. **Invest in evaluation infrastructure sooner.** The Docker pain was partly because we set it up properly only late in the competition. Getting the containerised eval loop working in week one would have saved hours.
3. **More aggressive reward shaping from day one.** The final reward function was the product of failure analysis. Running a set of deliberate ablations earlier would have surfaced the failure modes faster.

## What it took

Six weeks, four architectural approaches, one sleepless night, and a teammate willing to stare at loss curves alongside you until dawn.

The RL challenge at Brainhack is legitimately hard. The environment is adversarial, the observability is constrained, and the evaluation is unforgiving. Getting a PPO+LSTM agent to the point where it navigates intelligently — collecting points, anticipating guards, exploring efficiently — required not just the right architecture but the right reward signal, the right training time, and the right willingness to throw away what was not working.

Top 16. A result I'll keep building on.
